Minorities face disproportionate impact from COVID-19
by Anton Van De Motter

As the United States continues to grapple with COVID-19, racial disparities are emerging in both cases and the economic fallout. Minority workers are experiencing higher rates of unemployment and little change even as the rates dip. They are also facing higher rates of hospitalization for COVID-19 related illness.

Unemployment

According the Bureau of Labor Statistics, unemployment rates are dropping. How much they drop depends on who you are. As White persons return to work, minority workers are seeing little change. Minority workers are disproportionately represented in low-income service jobs. Many of the businesses they work for faced restrictions to stop the spread of COVID-19, leading to mass unemployment and layoffs. For some, the situation isnâ€™t getting any better. The amount of permanent job loss has increased. The unemployment rate has actually risen for Black or African American men.

The number of unemployed persons who were on temporary layoff decreased by 4.8 million in June to a total of 10.6 million. The number of permanent job losers increased by 588,000 to a total of 2.9 million.

Unemployment rates decreased overall for White, Black or African American, Asian, and Hispanic populations.

White and Hispanic persons saw a greater decrease in unemployment compared to Black or African American and Asian persons.

When seperated by sex, the unemployment rate increased for Black or African American men.
